Maximum Area of a Triangle

Dacian
2022-01-12
#Problems

What is the maximum area of a triangle inscribed in a circle?

It is almost obvious that it would be an equilateral triangle to obtain the maximum area. Here is a quick way to prove it.

We prove it by contradiction:

Assume there exisits a triangle that satisfies the requirements of the problem but is not an equilateral triangle, i.e., at least one vertex is not on the perpendicular bisector of its opposite side.

Then we can derive that, if we move this vertex to the intersection point of the perpendicular bisector and the corresponding arc, we can obtain a new triangle with a larger area, which contradicts the assumption.

Thus the hypothesis is not valid, which means there is not any triangle that satisfies the requirements of the problem but is not an equilateral triangle.

But it is not finished yet. We now know that triangles with a maximum area must not be equilateral triangles, does it mean they are then equilateral triangles? May not. We should first make sure that, there are triangles with a maximum area. I am not sure how to strictly clarify it, I can just say it is “obvious” or the question itself implies they exsit . Let’s assume it is true.

Finally, we know that, (1) there are triangles with a maximum area, and (2) Such triangles are not scalene triangles. Because triangles consist of scalene triangles and equilateral triangles, then triangles that satisfy the requirements of the problem are equilateral triangles.

This idea is simple and interesting. When I first saw this problem, I came with another idea.

My overall idea is: I try to prove for any scalene triangle S, we can find a S* which is larger than S but smaller than the equilateral triangle. If it is proved, then we can conclude, the triangle with the maximum area is an equilateral triangle.

First, it is obvious that, given an equilateral triangle inscribed in a circle, if we fix two vertices and move the other, its area decreases as its height deceases while the corresponding side is not changed. Note that the moving vertex angle is always 60 degrees.

Then we know that, for any triangle inscribed in a circle, as long as one of its angles is 60 degrees, its area must be smaller than (or equal to) an equilateral triangle.

For any scalene triangle S, I can find such a S* that S* > S and one of its angles is 60 degrees.

Here is how I find it:



For any triganle, as least one angle is less than 60. Assume it is angle D. First we move D -> F, is it obvious that the ares is increasing. Then angle C in CFE is larger than 60 as angle F is smaller than 60. Then we move E -> G, during which time the area is still increasing (because we are increasing its height by turning it to a isosceles triangle) and there must one moment when angle C = 60 (this is the S we want*) because angle C is smaller than 60 in CFE and larger than 60 in CGF.

Both two ideas start from guessing the conclusion first. But the second one tells the story in a bottom to top manner, while the first one directly makes it on top.
